 Introduction
Imagine a novice intern in one of the clinics in Astana, who can ask for help from two different digital assistants in analyzing a complex clinical case. “Oracle”, the first assistant, is a traditional model recommend-and-refer: it instantly issues a diagnosis and selects arguments solely in favor of its correctness (Miller, 2023). This approach is based on the "alethic nature" of AI, which suggests that a machine always tells the truth, limiting autonomy in decision-making of a doctor (Longo et al., 2024). “Critic”, the second assistant embodies the evaluative AI paradigm: it shows arguments “for” and “against” of the hypothesizes proposed by an intern (Miller, 2023). The ability to evaluate judgments’ strengths and weaknesses critically is the fundamental characteristic of expert thinking in medicine (Miller, 2023). Which of these tools will provide a safer and more informed calibration of trust among the future generation of doctors?
The question is significant for Kazakhstan to implement the Digital Kazakhstan state program aimed at creating a high-tech industry of the future (Government of the Republic of Kazakhstan, 2017). AI is officially recognized as a strategic “opportunity” in the SWOT analysis of the national health system until 2025 (Government of the Republic of Kazakhstan, 2019). Practical efficiency of such technologies in Kazakhstan is confirmed: pilot projects on remote monitoring have reduced the number of hospital admissions by 57% (Mukhammedzhanova & Suleimenova, 2024). However, the success of digital transformation depends not only on algorithmic accuracy but on the user trust (Alowais et al., 2023).
In the socio-cultural context of Kazakhstan, the trust issue is complicated by the historical legacy of the centralized Semashko system (Lim et al., 2025). This hierarchical structure has formed a monologue model of communication, where information is translated primarily from top to bottom (Lim et al., 2025). In this environment, AI systems might be perceived by students as indisputable authority (Longo et al., 2024). The lack of transparency in government decision-making leads to a modern lack of trust in official sources of information (Lim et al., 2025). Without changing the paradigm of interaction with technology, future specialists may face systemic difficulties in interpreting clinical advice (Lim et al., 2025). 
Research in HCI shows that the presence of convincing but superficial explanations often provokes overreliance (excessive trust) to AI systems (Buçinca et al., 2021). In the experiment with the 199 participants, it was proved that users tend to perceive erroneous AI advice if the interface does not stimulate analytical thinking (Buçinca et al., 2021). The large-scale meta-analysis of 106 unique papers confirmed that human-AI interaction often demonstrates negative synergy (Vaccaro et al., 2024). Verifiability (the explanation’s ability to provide the doctor with evidence for independent verification of the algorithm's output) becomes the critical factor of success (Fok & Weld, 2024); without this mechanism AI adoption might lead to deskilling (degradation of clinical skills) of the new generation of doctors (Alowais et al., 2023). It is necessary to create systems that engage a user’s slow, analytical thinking (Buçinca et al., 2021).
The choice of this research question is based on three fundamental arguments. Firstly, in publications about countries with low- and middle-income level AI becomes to be more under consideration as an instrument for healthcare systems’ strengthening (Ciecierski-Holmes et al., 2022). Meanwhile, empirical base of such works is mainly formed by research in in upper-middle income countries (whose situation is close to Kazakhstan’s) (Ciecierski-Holmes et al., 2022). The review by Ciecierski-Holmes et al. (2022) includes examples of China and several other LMIC regions (like Latin America, South Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa), which creates the base for transferring relevant practices to Kazakhstan. Secondly, AI Explainability requirements in clinical systems are defined by a goal: to decide what should be explained to a user (Markus et al., 2021). Therefore, the choice of the explanation should relate to a certain framework, which helps reasonably choose between classes of explainable AI methods depending on the clinical task and the user's needs (Markus et al., 2021). Since the lack of transparency is described as one of the key barriers of implementation, and confidence in system’s reliability is crucial for clinicians, choosing the most relevant type of explanation remains critical for trust and acceptance (Markus et al., 2021). Thirdly, AI in medicine can support clinical practice, including diagnostics, personalized treatment and help in decision making (Alowais et al., 2023). Systems of decision-making support in the basis of AI are capable to provide recommendations in the regime, close to real-time, contributing to choosing diagnostical and therapeutic actions (Alowais et al., 2023). If there is enough explanation of recommendations, the opportunity to evaluate patient’s situation correctly and create accurate treatment plan strengthens practical benefits of AI in clinical context (Alowais et al., 2023).
This study aims to determine whether the directive logic of the “Oracle” or the evaluative support of the “Critic” is more conducive to building strategic trust among medical students in Kazakhstan. By testing these paradigms under varying levels of AI accuracy, the research seeks to establish the first empirically grounded guidelines for XAI implementation in Kazakhstan’s clinical education.
